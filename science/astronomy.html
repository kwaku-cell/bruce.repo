Introduction

Astronomy is the scientific study of celestial objects such as stars, planets, comets, and galaxies, as well as phenomena that occur outside Earth's atmosphere. It is one of the oldest sciences, with roots in ancient civilizations that observed the night sky to track time, navigate, and understand their place in the universe.

Modern astronomy is divided into observational and theoretical branches, with advancements in technology allowing us to explore the universe in unprecedented detail.

History of Astronomy

The history of astronomy dates back thousands of years. Ancient civilizations such as the Babylonians, Egyptians, and Greeks made significant contributions to our understanding of the cosmos. The invention of the telescope in the 17th century revolutionized astronomy, allowing scientists like Galileo Galilei to observe celestial objects in greater detail.

In the 20th century, space exploration and the development of advanced telescopes, such as the Hubble Space Telescope, expanded our knowledge of the universe. Today, astronomy continues to be a field of discovery, with missions to study distant planets, stars, and galaxies.

Modern Astronomy

Modern astronomy relies on cutting-edge technology and interdisciplinary research. Observatories equipped with powerful telescopes, both on Earth and in space, allow astronomers to study the universe across the electromagnetic spectrum. Radio, infrared, ultraviolet, X-ray, and gamma-ray astronomy provide insights into phenomena that are invisible to the naked eye.

Space missions, such as the James Webb Space Telescope and Mars rovers, are uncovering new information about the origins of the universe, the potential for life on other planets, and the nature of dark matter and dark energy.
